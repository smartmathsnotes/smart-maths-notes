EXPONENTS AND LOGARITHMS

A.EXPONENTS

1. Definition

Exponents (or Indices) show how many times a number is multiplied by itself.

example;

x3 = x · x · x

2. Basic Laws of Exponents

(i) Multiplication Law

To multiply powers with the same base, keep the base and add the exponents.

xᵃ · xᵇ = xᵃ⁺ᵇ

(ii) Division Law

To divide powers with the same base, keep the base and subtract the exponents.

xᵃ ÷ xᵇ = xᵃ⁻ᵇ

(iii) Power of a Power Law

To raise a power to another power, keep the base and multiply the exponents.

(xᵃ)ᵇ = xᵃᵇ

(iv) Power of a Product Law

When a product is raised to a power, apply that power to every factor inside the parentheses.

(xy)ᵃ = xᵃyᵃ

(v) Zero Exponent Law

Any non-zero number raised to the power of zero is always equal to 1.

x⁰ = 1

(vi) Negative Exponent Law

A negative exponent indicates the reciprocal (one over) of the base with a positive exponent.

x⁻ᵃ = 1 / xᵃ

(vii) Fractional Exponent Law

The denominator of a fractional exponent indicates the root, while the numerator indicates the power.

x1/2 = √x

xm/n = ⁿ√(xᵐ)

3. Examples

(i) x² · x³ = x⁵

(ii) x⁶ ÷ x² = x⁴

(iii) (x²)³ = x⁶

(iv) x⁻² = 1/x²

(v) x1/2 = √x

TAKE NOTE:

  • Bases must be identical: You can only add or subtract the powers if the main numbers (the bases) are exactly the same.
  • Addition is not multiplication: There is no rule to simplify exponents when you are adding or subtracting terms; index laws only apply to multiplication and division.
  • Zero power equals one: Any number (except zero) raised to the power of zero will always be 1, no matter how large the number is.
  • Negative powers make fractions: A negative sign in the exponent does not make the number negative; it simply tells you to flip the number into a fraction.

B. LOGARITHMS

1. Definition

A logarithm tells us the power to which a base must be raised to get a number.

2. General Form

logₐ(b) = x

where :

  • a = Base
  • x = Logarithm (Result)
  • b = Argument

The above logarithm is read as:

This is read as : Log "b" base "a" is equal to "x".

MEANING : "x" is the "Logarithm of Argument "b" base "a".

Example

log₁₀(100) = 2

This is read as : Log "100" base "10" is equal to "2".

Meaning ;

10² = 100

This is now in Exponent Form.

3. Changing Between Forms

Logarithm Form can be changed into Exponent Form and vice versa :

(i) Changing From Logarithm Form to Exponent Form

When changing Logarithm Form to Exponent Form :

  • The "base" remains constant (It does not change)
  • The "Logarithm" becomes the "Power"
  • The "Argument" becomes the "Result"

In simple terms :

We simply swap the "Argument" and the "Logarithm", then remove log.

Example

Convert the following Logarithm Form to Exponent Form :

logₐ(b) = x

In this Logarithm :

  • a = Base
  • x = Logarithm (Result)
  • b = Argument

When converted it to Exponent Form, it becomes :

ax = b

Where :

  • a = Base
  • x = Power (Exponent)
  • b = Result

(ii) Changing From Exponent Form to Logarithm Form

When changing an Exponent Form to logarithm form :

  • The "base" remains constant (It does not change)
  • The "power" becomes the "Logarithm" (Result)
  • The "Result" becomes the "Argument"

In simple terms :

We simply swap the "Power" and the "Result", then introduce log.

Example

Convert the following Exponent Form to Logarithm Form :

aˣ = b

In this Exponent Form :

"a" is the "base"

"x" is the "power"

"b" is the "result"

When converted it to Logarithm Form, it becomes :

logₐ(b) = x

Where :

  • a = Base
  • x = Logarithm (Result)
  • b = Argument

Examples

(i) 2³ = 8

👉 log₂(8) = 3

(ii) 10² = 100

👉 log₁₀(100) = 2

(iii) 3³ = 27

👉 log₃(27) = 3

TAKE NOTE :

When Converting Logarithms to Exponents and vice versa :

  • The base stays the base: The small number at the bottom of a logarithm always remains the main large number (the base) when you switch to index form.
  • The answer and the power swap: When moving between forms, the number that was the "answer" in one form becomes the "power" in the other.
  • Logs always find the exponent: Remember that a logarithm is simply a mathematical way of asking "to what power" a number must be raised.
  • Arguments must be positive: You can only take the logarithm of a positive number; you cannot have a log of zero or a negative number.
